Mean Reversion: Trading the Snapback

6 min read

Mean reversion is the idea that prices and returns tend to move back toward their historical average after an extreme move. Traders use it to spot when an asset looks “stretched” relative to its recent mean, then look for a reversal back toward normal.

In crypto, mean reversion shows up constantly—especially in liquid markets—because leverage, liquidations, and crowd positioning can push price away from fair value briefly, then snap back when pressure fades.

What Mean Reversion Is (and What It Isn’t)

Core idea

When price deviates far from a reference “mean” (like a moving average), it often reverts toward it over time. Mean reversion is strongest in range-bound markets and weakest in strong trends.

Biggest danger: catching a falling knife

A move that looks “overextended” can stay overextended if the market is trending or news-driven. Your strategy must define what counts as a regime change and when to stop trading.

Choosing Your “Mean”

The “mean” is just a reference level you expect price to revisit. Common choices:

  • Moving average (SMA/EMA) — e.g., 20, 50, or 200 period averages
  • VWAP — popular for intraday reversion (especially on high-volume pairs)
  • Statistical mean / z-score — define extreme moves as deviations (e.g., z-score ±2)

Signals: How Traders Identify “Stretched” Price

Mean reversion strategies usually combine a “stretch” signal and a “reversal” confirmation to reduce false entries.

Common signal stack

  • Bollinger Bands — price touches/exceeds outer band, then closes back inside
  • RSI — overbought/oversold thresholds (e.g., > 75 or < 25 in short-term systems)
  • Volatility filter — avoid trading during abnormal spikes where spreads and slippage explode

A simple rule template (Bollinger + RSI)

One example approach: enter only after price shows an extreme and then “fails” to continue.

  • Long: candle low touches/breaks lower band, then closes back above it, with RSI low
  • Short: candle high touches/breaks upper band, then closes back below it, with RSI high

Exits and Risk Controls

Mean reversion strategies win by taking many small edges. That means:

  • Keep stops explicit — define where the idea is invalidated
  • Take profits systematically — common target is the mean (middle band / moving average)
  • Account for costs — frequent trading makes fees and slippage a first-class concern
